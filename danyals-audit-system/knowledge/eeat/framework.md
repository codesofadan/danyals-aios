# E-E-A-T framework - operational version

Loaded by: A1 (Content + E-E-A-T).
Sources: Google QRG; Lily Ray commentary; Marie Haynes QRG breakdowns; Aleyda Solis's E-E-A-T playbook.

## What the four letters mean in plain terms

- **E - Experience**: has the author actually used / done / been-there? First-hand signals.
- **E - Expertise**: has the author trained, studied, or worked in this field?
- **A - Authoritativeness**: do others in the field treat this site/author as a reliable source?
- **T - Trust**: is the page honest, accurate, safe, and clear about who runs it?

Trust is the umbrella. A page can have expertise without trust (a credentialed scammer); the opposite is rare.

## Audit signals per dimension

### Experience signals to look for
- Photos showing the author in the place / using the product
- Dates and specifics ("I installed this geyser on 14 March 2025, here is the invoice")
- First-person voice with concrete details
- User-generated context (Q&A, reviews, comments)

### Expertise signals
- Author bylines with credentials and dated career history
- Author bio links to verifiable profiles (LinkedIn, professional registries, academic pages)
- In-depth, technically correct content (no shallow definitions of advanced terms)
- Explained tradeoffs rather than blanket recommendations

### Authoritativeness signals
- Inbound links from peers and industry sites (off-page audit)
- Brand mentions on industry publications, Wikipedia, knowledge graph
- Co-citation: the site is named in lists of "best resources for X"
- Author has spoken/written elsewhere; auditable speaker bios, podcasts, books

### Trust signals
- HTTPS + secure handling of any user input
- Visible ownership: about page with founder names + addresses + photos
- Clear contact info: phone, email, address (NAP)
- Policies: refund, returns, privacy, terms - dated and accessible
- No deceptive UX patterns (fake countdown timers, unclear pricing, hidden auto-renewal)
- For local businesses: matching NAP across GBP + on-site + tier-1 citations

## Anti-signals (red flags)

- Anonymous "team" without any named individuals on commercial content
- "About us" page that talks only about benefits, not about the people
- No phone or address for a service business
- Reviews that look templated, all dated within a narrow window, or all 5-star
- Author bio that links to a generic LinkedIn with no relevant work history
- Content that contradicts known authoritative sources without explanation

## YMYL adjustment

Topics that materially affect health, safety, finances, or major life decisions raise the bar on every signal. A health page with no medical-professional author or review is a critical failure where a recipe page with no author byline is only a minor one.

## How A1 emits findings using this framework

A1 cites the specific signal that is present or missing. "Author block on the article cites Dr Ali Khan, no link to a verifiable profile, no dated credentials - expertise signal weak. Severity major (not critical) because the article is on home decor, not YMYL."
